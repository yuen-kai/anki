Preliminary  
The mental model that ties it together: a serious MCAT studier runs three loops simultaneously — learn it (content review), retain it (Anki spaced repetition), and apply it under test conditions (QBank \+ full-lengths) — with the QBank and full-lengths feeding a feedback signal that redirects the other two.

For the *science* sections specifically, dedicated "how to apply" instruction is thin. The dominant cultural advice in MCAT prep: "learn the content, then do a ton of passages and read every explanation, and the application skill will emerge." It does emerge for most people, because the explanations are good and the volume is high, but it emerges somewhat inductively rather than being taught top-down as a curriculum of reasoning skills. There isn't a widely-used product that says "here is a systematic course in *applying* biochemistry to novel experimental setups" the way there's a clear course in *learning* biochemistry.

### **Purpose**

Build an evidence-based picture of how transfer of learning works and which study and instructional strategies actually produce it — specifically the move from knowing MCAT/biology content to applying it under novel, test-like conditions.

### **In-scope**

* **How people actually study** — the realistic study loop, how time gets allocated across sections, how prep is sequenced over a timeline, and how approaches differ between higher and lower performers.  
* **The tool landscape, by function** — what each category of tool does and what it's actually used for: question banks, spaced-repetition decks, full-length practice tests, content review, study planners. Function and adoption, not marketing.  
* **The learning science** — the principles that bear on this kind of studying: spaced repetition and its scheduling algorithms, retrieval practice and the testing effect, interleaving, desirable difficulties, the worked-example effect, deliberate practice, metacognition and error analysis, and transfer of learning (near vs. far transfer).  
* **How to learn/remember a ton of information**  
* **How application is learned** — how studiers move from knowing content to applying it under test conditions, what currently teaches that transition, and what the transfer literature says about whether and how it can be taught.  
* **Predictive validity** — what actually predicts MCAT performance, what the strongest ground-truth signals are, and what they correlate with.

1. Gick & Holyoak (1983), Schema Induction and Analogical Transfer, Cognitive Psychology 15, 1–38 — [https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0010028583900026](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/0010028583900026).  
   * Two contrasting analogs \+ abstraction strategies → induced schema –(predicitve of)→ transfer  
     * One problem \+ abstraction strategies (summarization instructions, a verbal statement of the underlying principle, or a diagrammatic representation) \= ineffective  
     * two contrasting analogs \-\> derived a problem schema as an incidental product of describing the similarities of the analogs  
       * Furthermore, the verbal statements and diagrams that had failed to facilitate transfer from one analog proved highly beneficial when paired with two.  
     * The quality of the induced schema was highly predictive of subsequent transfer performance.

Experts

Expert 1 — Keith J. Holyoak

Who: Distinguished Professor of Psychology, UCLA; co-author (with Mary Gick) of the foundational analogical-transfer studies.

Focus: Analogical reasoning and schema induction — how people map a known problem onto a novel one. Known for the finding that a single example transfers poorly, but comparing two analogs lets learners abstract a general schema that drives transfer.

Why Follow: Gick & Holyoak is the empirical bedrock for the whole "compare cases to build deep structure" mechanism in this BrainLift — why varied, contrasting practice (not single examples) is what produces transfer, and why surface features mislead. It directly informs the application-scaffolding argument and the "identify the underlying principle through a disguised surface" core of the MCAT analysis.

Where: UCLA Reasoning Lab https://reasoninglab.psych.ucla.edu/ · Google Scholar: search "Keith Holyoak" (verify the profile).

Expert 2 — José P. Mestre

Who: Emeritus Professor of Physics and Educational Psychology, University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign; with Robert Dufresne, William Gerace, and Pamela Hardiman in the physics-education-research group.

Focus: Expert–novice problem solving; forcing novices through principle-first, hierarchical problem analysis (which principle → which concept → only then which procedure) to shift them from surface-feature to deep-structure representations.

Why Follow: The Dufresne, Gerace, Hardiman & Mestre (1992) study is the template for the application scaffold — explicitly training the "identify the principle first" step instead of leaving it implicit in volume practice. Their result that this produces more expert-like deep-structure judgments and better problem solving is the closest direct evidence for the design.

Where: Illinois Experts https://experts.illinois.edu/en/persons/jose-p-mestre · Google Scholar: search "Jose P. Mestre" (verify the profile).
